I began identifying as vegan on January 1st, 2024. Now that my 2-year Veganiversary has passed, I'd like to write a little about how I became this way, how it's going, what my diet looks like, and a number of common talking points that are often raised in discussions of veganism. I'll do my best not to be preachy or sanctimonious.

### What is veganism?
Here's the Vegan Society's definition of veganism. I'm too lazy to provide my own definition, and also it's useful to include this here because I'll be referencing it later.

> Veganism is a philosophy and way of living which seeks to exclude - as far as is possible and practicable - all forms of exploitation of, and cruelty to, animals for food, clothing or any other purpose.

In other words, vegans try not to consume animal products like meat, milk, cheese and eggs. They typically avoid buying shoes, jackets and furniture made from animal materials like skin and wool, and they don't support animal-based entertainment like horse- and dog-racing. So it's not just a diet - it's a lifestyle that seeks to minimise animal suffering.

### My journey to veganity
Unlike some vegans and vegetarians, I didn't adopt this philosophy overnight, but instead went through a gradual transformation. It's surprisingly difficult to identify the seeds that set the transformation in motion, but here's my best attempt at untangling it.

For a long time, I had the vague sense that my meat-eating habit wasn't based on a particularly solid moral foundation. The difference in the treatment of "pets" and "farm animals" was probably contradictory, I thought. And in the future, when all our nutritional needs would be met by lab-produced pills, and we could experience any possible sensory delight through the control of our brain interfaces, then surely the habit of killing animals and eating their meat would be considered a barbaric and pointlessly cruel practice. The vegetarians and vegans, I said to myself, were on the right side of history.

But there's a difference between having a vague awareness of something, and feeling the need to act on it. I was comfortable in my decades-long custom of buying strips of animal flesh ("meat", if you will), frying them in a pan to remove the bacteria, and eating them. I liked eggs in all their forms: scrambled, fried, poached, or in an omelette. Chocolate and ice-cream were my sweets of choice, and I certainly have a sweet tooth. Cheese pizza was perhaps my favourite food to eat at restaurants, and I'd developed a taste for proper stonebaked ones. I happily drank standalone glasses of milk. As for steak - "medium, please!". I didn't think about how these foods were produced.

The turning point was when I started my PhD. Over half of the other students who joined the same program were vegetarian or vegan. None of them ever tried to proselythise me, but through observing them and through casual lunchtime conversation, I became conscious of my decades-long habit, and of the fact that I could change it if I wanted to. I guess I was curious and asked them about their diets.

Then, somewhat whimsically, and without much moral conviction behind it, I took up the challenge of cooking more vegetarian meals at home. It seemed like a generally *good* thing to do. Good for the planet, good for animals, and probably good for my health. Why not, then? To begin with, I swapped out ingredients in my existing recipes. For example, my beef stir fry became a vegetable stir fry. Beans and lentils were adopted into my roster of ingredients. (TODO check recipe book, did I learn new recipes?)

Before long, meat ended up being entirely eliminated from my cooking, and my diet was ~95% vegetarian. I was reluctant to commit to full vegetarianism, as I didn't want to limit my options when eating out at restaurants. There was a honey-glazed chicken burger that I especially didn't want to give up. I was scared by the prospect of applying a definitive label like  "vegetarian" to myself, and also, I didn't want to inconvenience my parents when visiting home.

The next step in my journey was that I started dating a vegan. "Something you should probably know is that I'm 95% vegetarian," I said during our first ever conversation, thinking, based on her naturey vibes, that it would impress her. "Oh, that's okay, I'm vegan," she replied, disdainfully (not really). She introduced me to oat milk, which soon became the only type of milk I drank, because it was tastier than cow milk. She also introduced me to tofu, which became another "meat substitute" in my diet. And, she brought me to lots of vegan-friendly restaurants, and gradually I convinced myself that it was okay to let go of the final 5%.

I'm not sure when was the last time I intentionally ate meat. I remember there was an International Lunch in my university department, where each student would bring in a dish from their culture, and I decided to partake of the meat-based dishes. After the fact, however, I felt grossed out by what I had done. Eating meat no longer seemed like a normal thing to do. It wasn't compartmentalised like it was before, and I found it hard not to think of animals as I ate.

### Vegan FAQ
As a vegan, you find yourself having the same tedious conversations with non-vegans about veganism, over and over and over again. It's the equivalent of a tall person being told "wow, you're tall" every time they meet someone  new. For this reason, I hesitate to reveal to anyone that I'm vegan, but it tends to be dragged out into the open when food is being eaten or discussed.

Here, I'll cover the common talking points that I've encountered since becoming vegan.

* "Wow, that must be so hard, I could never do that"
* YOU NEED PROTEIN AND CALCIUM OR YOU'LL DIE (b12)
* You broke your arm because you're vegan (post hoc ergo propter hoc)
* Well aren't you a sanctimonious little prick
* Automatic Defensiveness (vegans existing is perceived as a challenge)
* Mr. Save the Planet is still taking flights (environmental reasons; initially said that because I didn't want my family to feel under attack; my defence is now "you're not a nun so you must not believe in God")
* But animals in Ireland are treated well... (cow getting kicked; they still have to be killed; and dairy cows still have their babies taken away)
* Goat farmers in the Sahara desert can't be vegan (cultural erasure)
* But almonds, deforestation, and whatnot! And veggie farming kills wildlife too... (simple refutation: farm animals need to be fed veggies too; not sure how to deal w/ the idea that they're given waste products to eat)
* Do you miss any foods? (chocolate, sweets (gelatine), ice-cream)
* Is it hard to find vegan options? (in Ireland, no. Traveling, probably).
* Would you eat meat to survive?
* Would you feed your pets meat? (dogs no, cats yes)
* Would you eat insects?
* Is honey vegan?
* What would you do about pests?
* Shoes and belts are annoying (animal skin)
* It's annoying when something COULD be vegan but has unnecessary dairy
* What's the hardest part of being vegan? (getting flak from family)
* Chinese openness about where meat comes from
* Would you pick an egg off the ground and eat it?
* What happens to the animals when the farms are all shut down?

### Conclusions (would I recommend it)
Ultimately, I do think it's a more ethical way to live. I also think people should be free to make their own decisions. If your preference is to continue eating meat, then my challenge is for you to learn more about where that meat comes from etc. If you're still fine with it, then that's grand. If you're uncomfortable even at the thought of learning more, then maybe that's your conscience speaking to you.
